## What's aeroponics?

Aeroponics is a form of culture where plants are growing vertically in air or a mist environment. There is no soil, we use the water to distribute a nutrient solution to the roots.

There are multiple types of cultures with water, the major ones I know are :

- **Hydroponics** : The plants are growing with their roots directly exposed in a liquid nutrient solution. Some people use derivative techniques like supporting plants on clay pebbles.
- **Aquaponics** : Same as hydroponics but with a "natural" nutrient solution coming from an aquaculture (most of the time fishes). This one can be complex due to the aquaculture, but way more better in terms of nutrients.
- **Aeroponics** : It can be performed in a low-pressure or a high-pressure system. The roots take benefits of the air exposure and the micro-droplets of water containing the nutrient solution. It's known as a very efficient solution to grow plants vertically while improving the returns. This technique was studied by NASA.

By growing plants vertically you gain more space, you can produce more and the returns are improved by an highly oxygenated nutrient solution. The research conducted by NASA demonstrates that a droplet size inferior to 50 microns improve the nutrient solution absorption by the roots. That's why I decided to start with an high pressure system as my main goal is to get the most productive solution possible while optimizing the growing space.

<img src="/images/illustrations/construction.svg" alt="Construction illustration" style="width:50%; margin:auto" />

But there are three major drawbacks I see in high-pressure aeroponics system (HPA) :

1. **The complexity** : Finding the different parts that support high pressure can be really complex, you need to have food safe materials, think about the security, think about the automation and of course control the environment. Any failure in the system can damage completely your plants.
2. **The cost** : Depending on your system setup and where you are located, the price can vary. If just like me you live in France, then you will certainly need to import some material as it's not produced in this country. Say welcome to the taxes! What's the solution? Maybe 3D printing with food safe materials?
3. **Pollination** : Not only connected to HPA systems but to any controlled environments where there is no insects. While you can remove the pesticides from the equation, human will spend more time in doing what the nature normally do. I don't think we have efficient ways to automate this at the moment, but maybe things will change!

However I think the benefits are worth it as documented in the [research conducted by NASA and AgriHouse](https://spinoff.nasa.gov/Spinoff2006/er_2.html) :

- Water usage reduced by **98%** : We are using a closed circuit and we mist the roots by intervals.
- Pesticides usage reduced by **100%** : We are in a controlled environment so we can get rid of the pesticides that are killing insects and the pollination process.
- Fertilizer usage reduced by **60%**
- Maximize crop yields by **45% to 75%**

And finally one paragraph caught my attention in the NASA article, inlined with what I want to achieve :

> By conserving water and eliminating harmful pesticides and fertilizers used in soil, growers are doing their part to protect the Earth. Moreover, the NASA SBIR results demonstrated that this aeroponic technology delivers an 80-percent increase in dry weight biomass per square meter, when compared to hydroponic and soil-based growing techniques

## Agriculture : what's wrong?

### Intensive agriculture

It's not a secret, we will face major challenges to feed the worldwide population while keeping low our impact on the environment. Sadly, while I'm writing this blog post, there is an ongoing war between Russia and Ukraine. The recent sanctions against Russia literally changed our world and will have durable impacts on global food security. But I don't want to talk too much about it as we still have a lot of incertitudes and that's not what drove me in the creation of this aeroponics farming project. Instead, we will focus on a few key numbers related to the impacts of intensive agriculture.

_Our World in Data_ published an interesting article [^food-ghg-emissions] where we learn that **crop production represents 27% of food emissions** with fetilizers and agricultural machinery participating to this. HPA systems can help to reduce the use of fertilizers as we saw previously by 60%. The machinery can also be optimized for vertical farming as everything is more condensed. The author also mentions that **land use for crops for human consumption represents 8% of food emissions**, which is in my opinion something vertical farming, coupled to the right systems, can fix. Instead of extending the production horizontally you can make good use of height and maximise crop yields.

[^food-ghg-emissions]: [Food production is responsible for one-quarter of the world’s greenhouse gas emissions](<(https://ourworldindata.org/food-ghg-emissions)>), Hannah Ritchie, November 06 2019

<img src="/images/illustrations/plant_soil.svg" alt="Plant in hands illustration" style="width:40%; margin:auto" />

_Grantham Centre - for Sustainable Futures_ also published a paper, _A sustainable model for intensive agriculture_ [^sustainable-model-intensive-agriculture], bringing some key figures denoting the disaster of this type of agriculture for the land. We learn that **"nearly 33% of the world’s arable land has been lost to erosion or pollution in the last 40 years"**. We already consumed our resources and I personnally don't think we will improve on it. We continue to deforest and deplete our soils. I want to quote a part of the paper since it resonates so much to me and summarize the situation of our plants and soils :

> Elite modern crops are optimised for a system of high- nutrient artificial inputs and chemical control of pests and diseases; they have consequently lost their natural reliance on microbes to extract complex nutrients from the soil and for defence against natural enemies. Soil is becoming a hydroponic system [...]

We need to adapt our techniques, give back the land to the nature and let it regenerates its fauna and flora. Vertical farming will help to achieve this objective, and controlled environment will stop the use of pesticides by 100%. However we will not remove chemicals and continue to grow plants that are weak naturally. That's why I'm convinced that we need to use the land in a healthy and intelligent way, for example by doing permaculture along vertical farming which is a temporary solution. Just like [The Ocean Cleanup](https://theoceancleanup.com/) team who clean the oceans from plastic pollution while they know the only solution is a deep change in our habits and the use of plastic.

### Nutrient pollution

Finally I just want to have this quick paragraph about nutrient pollution. It will be an issue in all types of agriculture making use of nutrients. For the moment I don't know how I can achieve a perfect nutrient consumption where plants absorb 100% of the solution. A lot of adjustments and knowledge will be necessary before running a system without any waste, but I'm optimistic since I'm not using any soil. I will try to follow this plan to reduce the pollution at maximum :

1. Manual tests : during the first tests mistakes will be made and I'll certainly end up with an unbalanced nutrient solution for my plants. I will try to reuse it as much as possible since the system is a closed water circuit. If it's not possible I plan to find disposal companies for dirty water, but this should be a last resort.
2. Iterate : still in a test phase but I will learn and optimize (per plant) more and more and eventually get a balanced nutrient solution that can be totally absorbed.
3. Monitor and automate : in order to deliver the right quantity at the right time. If you wonder how, I recommend this excellent video of the [automated hydroponic system build](https://youtu.be/nyqykZK2Ev4) made by Kyle Gabriel.

## High Pressure Aeroponic : System parts

Before setting up my high pressure aeroponic system, I did some research and created a small
infography to help me. You can consult it along the list of the main components configured for a working pressure of 100 PSI.

<a href="/images/blog/hpa_the_project/schema_hpa_system.png">
    <img src="/images/blog/hpa_the_project/schema_hpa_system_miniature.png" alt="Miniature of HPA system" style="margin:auto;" class="border border-red-600" />
</a>

- **Grow chamber** : This is where your plants will grow vertically without soil and in a mist environment. It needs enough space to ensure a good oxygenation of the plants roots. I recommend an insulating thermal barrier made of reflective foil, it will help you to control the temperature and maximize the light between the plants.

- **Black tubing** : Generally open diameter (OD) of 1/4" is recommended (and not less than 1/8" ID). This is because you want to get up to pressure as quickly as possible and reduce the use of your pump. The water flow is not really important in our system since the nozzles will not allow a large flow per seconds. However pressure really matters because you want an effective droplet size. The reason you need black tubes is to block light and prevent algae or bacteria growth, which should also help to prevent clogging. Finally make sure you have a working pressure corresponding to the needs of your system and keep in mind that the temperature influences it.

- **High pressure pump** : It delivers the nutrient solution to the system. You need a pump capable to produce a pressure of 80 PSI at least. Higher is better, it's easier to lower the pressure in your system.

- **Pressure switch** : It controls when the pump starts (cut-in at 80 PSI) and when it stops (cut-off at 100 PSI). The pump is just here to pressurize the water into the system, so no need to run it continuously.

- **Check valve** : Place it after your pump outlet to prevent a backflow in your piping system. It will avoid any damage due to the pressure when stopping the pump.

- **Water pressure tank** : One of the most important piece in your system to increase the life of your pump. It will pressurize the water with a preset, here at 98 PSI (2 PSI under the pump pressure), so the pump only delivers what is needed. Be sure to choose a pressure tank with a max pressure higher to your working pressure so you don't take any risk. Ask a professional to install the pressure tank and control it regularly.

- **Relief valve** : Very important, place it as close to the pressure tank as possible. If your pressure switch fails and the pump continues to pressurize the water, the relief valve will release the water flow and will prevent your pressure tank from exploding. In my system I use a 125 PSI relief valve.

- **Presure gauge** : Just to control your pressure in your pressurized line.

- **Shut-off valves** : To close the water circuit and help you to purge your system.

- **Solenoid** : Normally closed, when it receives an electrical signal (at 12V) it opens and lets the water flow. Coupled to the recycle timer, this is what allows you to control the misting cycles.

- **Recycle Timer** : Configure it to control the selenoid. You can have different cycles for days and nights. I will start with a misting cycle of 5 seconds on and 3 minutes off. Then I will iterate to find the best configuration.

- **Nozzles** : These are very challenging to find. They need to have a working pressure between 80 and 100 PSI (better) and a droplet size lower or equals to 50 microns to deliver the nutrient solution in the most efficient way. But you will have to make sure your nozzles don't clog easily and it will depend on the material, the working pressure, the salt in your solution, the chemical reactions with the other parts of your system, the temperature, ... so many things!

- **Fittings** : I recommend John Guest of DmFit fittings for the quality and the diversity of fittings. Also everything is food grade.

The real difficulty will be to find food grade materials for your system and the right fittings for the working pressure. Check the Youtube channel of [High Tech Gardener](https://www.youtube.com/channel/UCY-M5AiGnSfzOodfqGD3NgA), everything is perfectly explained!
